# Environmentally regulated methylation is related to individual fitness in a wild rodent population
Gina F. Lamka and Janna R. Willoughby

Environmental variation can shape phenotypic plasticity through epigenetic mechanisms such as DNA methylation, ultimately influencing fitness in wild populations. We examined methylation patterns in the banner-tailed kangaroo rat (Dipodomys spectabilis), a desert-adapted rodent with specialized sensory and physiological traits important for survival in arid environments. Using whole genome bisulfite sequencing of 42 individuals sampled across multiple years, we developed a within-year coefficient of variation filtering approach to identify methylation windows with low intra-annual variation among unrelated individuals. We interpret these regions as candidate loci whose methylation is shaped more by shared environmental conditions rather than genetic ancestry. These environmentally responsive windows were largely year-specific, suggesting that epigenetic plasticity, rather than long-term epigenetic memory, predominates under variable environmental conditions. Using lifetime reproductive success and longevity from the 17 generation pedigree, our ridge regression identified 93 methylation predictors of lifetime reproductive success and 60 associated with individual longevity. Although several regions overlapped annotated genes with functions consistent with processes influencing fitness, the majority of environmentally responsive windows occurred in noncoding regions, suggesting that regulatory elements in noncoding regions may contribute to the observed associations between methylation and fitness. Our results demonstrate that environmentally responsive methylation patterns are associated with fitness variation in a wild population and highlight the potential for epigenetic data to capture regulatory responses not reflected by genetic structure alone. More broadly, our approach provides a framework for identifying environment-associated epigenetic variation in systems with limited sample sizes, complex pedigrees, and temporally structured environmental variation.
